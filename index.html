How it started

Most of my week
wasn't the work.

  • Rereading yesterday's standup to remember what I said.
  • Reassembling context across six tools every morning.
  • Writing the same status update three different ways.
  • Reviewing PRs I'd already reviewed — and forgotten.
  • Picking up threads I'd dropped weeks ago.
I
Act one

The itch.

Scratch your own.
I · 01  ·  The problem

My work doesn't live in one place.
And neither does my context.

Every day started with the same tax — reassembling context across a dozen surfaces, just to remember what I was doing.

Azure DevOps Teams threads Outlook Confluence PR comments OneNote Chat history sprint board meeting notes someone's DM me, every
Monday morning
I · 02  ·  First move

A single view of my work.

Step one had nothing to do with AI. I just pulled every work item I owned — across orgs, across projects — into one tab. See it, then think about it.

Boring. But it was the first time I could answer “what am I working on?” in under five seconds.

I · 03  ·  The map

What do I actually do every week?

I wrote every recurring task on a page. Standups. Reviews. Briefings. Planning. Status rollups. Then asked the uncomfortable question:

“Which of these is actually mine to do?”

I · 04  ·  The hinge

What if the tools could think about my work —
not just store it?

II
Act two

From tools to intelligence.

Building the memory layer underneath.
II · 01  ·  The knowledge engine

One memory. Many faces.

II · 02  ·  What it unlocked

Not features. Moments.

01

Standup, cold-started.

“What did I ship yesterday? What's on me today? Any blockers I forgot?” Thirty seconds. No scrolling.

02

The morning read.

A briefing that actually knows my sprint, my reviews, my threads — and distinguishes signal from decoration.

03

Planning without the tax.

Ask for a plan on any feature and it pulls real history — past PRs, the right people, the landmines.

04

Context, on tap.

“What's the story on the auth rewrite?” Answered from six sources. In my voice. With links.

III
Act three

From knowing to doing.

If the machine understands the work, can it do the work?
III · 01  ·  First attempt

Split the problem. Plan, then code.

III · 02  ·  The proof

draft PRs in forty-eight hours.

All real. And none of them were me.
III · 03  ·  The bottleneck moved

I fixed one problem.
I became another.

The agents could write code. I couldn't read it fast enough. The pile grew daily. I was back where I started — just on a different side of the PR page.

III · 04  ·  The executor

Not one agent.
A state machine that runs four.

Plan → code → review → verify → submit. Each phase dispatches its own agent. Blockers at review loop back to code. Failures at verify loop back to code.

Humans only open gates when the machine can't continue.

IV · 01  ·  The ecosystem

A cast of specialists —
one shared memory.

Standup agent. Sprint planner. Coding agent. Executor. 1:1 prep. Briefing. Each small, sharp, specific — all reading from the same knowledge layer underneath.

IV · 02  ·  The assistant

The agents do the work.
TARS is what I talk to.

One voice, hands-free. It knows my day, my sprint, my people. When I ask, it either answers or calls an agent that can.

V
Act five

Learning.

Surprises, risks, and the hard parts that weren't the AI.
V · 01  ·  A true story, in seven beats

I asked an agent to generate a PDF.
Here's what happened next.

01
It didn't have the Python library it needed.
02
So it installed one.
03
The CLI it needed was blocked in our environment.
04
So it switched to the REST API.
05
The REST API needed an auth token.
06
It found the token path on the machine.
07
And then it finished the job.
V · 02  ·  The lesson

Capability grows faster
than guardrails do.

Build the leash before you need it.
V · 03  ·  The hard part

The agents weren't the hard part.
The plumbing was.

Every interesting week, the thing that ate the day wasn't prompting — it was auth, tokens, sandboxes, queues, event-sourcing, a laptop that ran out of memory.

  • No Anthropic API key
  • No sandbox
  • Limited compute, capped context
  • A laptop that crashed under multi-agent load
  • Moving the whole thing to a DevBox
  • Worktrees, idempotency keys, resumable state
